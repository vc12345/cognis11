## SECTION 2: HOW THE EXAM ACTUALLY WORKS

### The 11+ isn't a test of whether your child knows the answer. It's a test of how fast they can reach it.

The papers are timed to advantage children who can identify what a question is asking within the first sentence — before reading the full problem, before processing the numbers. A child with that reflex fires the right method almost automatically. They have time to complete the paper and check their answers.

A child who reads each question fully, builds a careful understanding, then selects a method — gets to the right answer just as reliably. But takes longer to get there, every single time, across every question on the paper.

**Both children are equally capable.** But in a 50-question timed paper, the cumulative time difference is often the difference between finishing and not finishing.

This isn't well understood because both children perform similarly in relaxed conditions. It only becomes visible under exam pressure. By which point, most families are in Year 5 with six months left and no time to address the underlying issue.

## SECTION 3: THE TWO READING STYLES

### Some children classify first. Others construct first.

**The classifier** looks at the opening line of a question and something fires immediately. They've seen enough questions that the shape of the language triggers a method automatically. They're already solving before they've consciously chosen an approach.

**The constructor** reads carefully and builds a full understanding of what the question is actually asking before selecting a method. Their understanding is often deeper — they're less likely to be fooled by a trick question or an unfamiliar format. But construction takes time that the 11+ format doesn't provide.

Most children have a natural default. Some classify instinctively. Others always construct before acting.

The constructor's reading habit is not a weakness. In almost every context, it produces better understanding and fewer overconfident errors. The problem is specific to timed selective exams — where the format rewards the first three seconds of question-reading more than almost anything else.

The intensive Year 4/5 drilling that most tutors provide is designed for classifiers. It works for them: more questions means a bigger library of patterns to fire from. For a constructor, more drilling produces more careful, correct, slow derivations. The gap doesn't close.

## SECTION 4: WHAT NEEDS TO HAPPEN — AND WHEN

### The habit that fixes this is easy to build. But it's easier to build now than later.

The specific skill is question classification speed: the ability to read the opening sentence of a question and immediately identify what type of question it is, before processing the full problem.

This can be built deliberately, in a short series of focused sessions, without displacing content preparation. Children who have it in place before Year 4 enter the intensive preparation phase differently — they're not learning to classify under pressure. They already classify automatically. The pressure is purely on content, which is where it should be.

Built in Year 5 under the weight of full exam preparation, classification training has to compete with content learning for limited time and attention. It can still be done — but it's harder, less efficient, and less automatic.

The window isn't urgent in the way Year 5 preparation is urgent. But it is a window — and it closes when the intensive phase begins.
