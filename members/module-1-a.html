Module 1: The Unitary Method

Foundation Intro

The Big Squash

The Unitary Method is a "Fair Share" game. Before we try to solve a big problem, we have to "squash" a group down to see what just one single item is worth. Imagine having a big pile of sweets and sharing them equally into boxes until the pile is gone.

"Look at this pile of 20 sweets. We have 4 boxes. If we share them out fairly, how many sweets end up in just one box? That's our 'Magic Number'."

The "Box Rule"

Tell your child: "The Unitary Method is just the number inside ONE box." Once we know the share for one box, we can calculate any number of boxes in the world!

Foundation Progressing

The Tug-of-War

When comparing two different "deals," the cost of one item acts like gravity. Whichever item has a higher price for just one unit is "heavier" on your wallet. We use the Tug-of-War to see which deal is pulling the most money away from us.

"Shop A and Shop B have different prices. But look at the scale—when we squash them both down to find the cost of one item, Shop A's unit is much heavier. It wins the tug-of-war because it costs more per piece!"

The "Tie-Breaker" Secret

A "Bigger Box" doesn't always mean a "Better Deal." The only way to break the tie and see the truth is to find the cost of one unit. The lighter unit always wins the 'Best Value' battle!

Supplemental Mastery

Phase 1: The X-Ray

This is the "Value of One" shortcut. In the 11+, children often panic when they see messy groups of numbers. Instead of guessing how they fit together, we always stop at a "bridge" halfway: finding the cost (or weight) of just one single item. Once they find "The One," they can scale up to any number the examiner throws at them with total confidence.

The Sweet Shop "If 4 apples cost 80p, how much do 6 apples cost?"
The Road Trip "A car travels 150 miles on 5 gallons. How far can it go on 8 gallons?"
The Heavy Crate "9 crates weigh 108kg. What is the mass of 13 crates?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

Point to the Unit Bridge below. We take the "Known Group" and slice it into equal pieces. By finding the Golden Unit (the gold square), we find the master key. This number never changes, no matter how many items we add later. Help your child notice that we shrink first to find the one, then grow to find the many.

1 Unit = £0.00

Phase 3: The Engine

Use the sliders with your child. Watch how the Golden Unit stays the same even as we change the "Target Quantity." The math always flows through that single unit cost.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Backwards Divide": Bright children often try to divide the smaller number by the bigger one (like $5 \div £20$) because it feels "easier" to fit them together. This leads to a messy decimal that makes no sense in a shop.

The Fix: "Money is Boss." Tell your child: "The total value (the money or weight) always goes into the calculator first." We always divide the Total Price by the Quantity. If they get a tiny result like $0.25$ for a cake that clearly costs more, ask them: "Does that price look like it belongs in a real shop?"
