The Sweet Share

A ratio isn't just a list of numbers; it's a Sharing Rule. If the rule is 2:1, it means every single time you give 2 sweets to the blue plate, you must give 1 sweet to the gold plate.

"We have a pile of sweets and a sharing rule. Let's follow the rule. Each 'round' of sharing uses up a certain number of sweets. Watch how many sets we can deal before the pile is empty!"

The Magic Envelope

To share a large number like £100, we don't deal individual coins. We find the Value of One Envelope. Once you know what's inside one envelope, you just hand them out to match the ratio.

"Look at these envelopes. The ratio 3:2 means we have 5 envelopes in total. If we split the whole £100 into those 5 envelopes, how much goes in each one? That's our magic number!"

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a ratio as a Chocolate Bar. To share it fairly, you don't just look at two people; you look at the total number of "chunks." The secret weapon for every ratio problem is finding the Value of One Chunk. Once your child knows what one single piece is worth, they can hand out the right amount to anyone.

Level 1 "Share £20 in the ratio 3:1. How much is the larger share?"
Level 2 "A 40cm rope is cut 5:3. How much longer is the first piece?"
Level 3 "A map scale is 2:7. If the total distance is 180km, find the smaller leg."

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Partition Bar. Every box inside this bar is identical. To find the "Value of One Box," you must divide the Total Amount by the Total Number of Boxes. Count the boxes below with your child—always find the "Total Parts" first.

Phase 3: The Engine

Use the sliders to test different scenarios. Watch how the Total Parts change the value of a single chunk. Remind your child: "The bar length stays the same, but the slices get smaller as we add more parts!"

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "One-Sided" Mistake: Bright children often divide the total by just one side of the ratio (e.g., $£100 \div 3$). They forget that the total covers the entire bar, not just one person's share.

The Fix: The "Add First" Rule. Always ask: "How many chunks are in the Whole Bar?" Add the ratio numbers together (A + B) before touching the division key. Once they have the value of one chunk, they are bulletproof.
