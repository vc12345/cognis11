Module 5: Direct Proportion

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Direct Proportion describes a relationship of Synchronous Movement. If one value increases, the other must increase by the exact same factor (the multiplier). In 11+ exams, the most reliable way to solve these is the Unitary Method: find the value of exactly "one" unit first, then multiply to find your target.

Advanced Year 2 "If 2 stickers cost 10p, how much do 6 stickers cost?"
Average Year 4 "A recipe for 4 people uses 300g of sugar. How much sugar is needed for 10 people?"
Advanced Year 5 "On a map with a scale of 1:25,000, a road is 8cm long. Calculate the actual length in kilometers."

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Additive" Trap: If a recipe for 4 people is scaled to 6 people, students often see the "extra 2 people" and simply add 2 to the ingredients. (e.g. 4 people = 10g, so 6 people = 12g).

The Fix: Use the Multiplier Check. Proportions are a multiplication game, never an addition game. If you add instead of multiply, the "strength" or "taste" of the ratio breaks. Always find the factor by which the quantity has grown ($Target \div Original$) and apply it to the value.
