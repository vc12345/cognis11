Module 36: Anagram Scrambler

Phase 1: The X-Ray

An anagram is formed by rearranging the letters of one word to produce another. To find them, you must look past the "sound" of the word and perform a Character Inventory. Two words are only valid anagrams if their sorted letter list—their "digital fingerprint"—is identical.

Advanced Year 2 "Are 'ACT' and 'CAT' anagrams? Count the letters in both to find the answer."
Average Year 4 "Rearrange the letters in 'LISTEN' to find a synonym for 'QUIET'."
Advanced Year 5 "Identify the outlier that is NOT an anagram of the others: 'RETAIN', 'REINA', 'TERNIA'."

Phase 2: The Anatomy

The Scramble Chamber. No matter how much you shake the tiles, the "Stock" (the letters available) remains unchanged. Anagrams are just different ways to arrange the same stock.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "One Letter Off" Trap: Mistakenly pairing words like DESSERT and DESERT because they "look" and "feel" identical. The brain assumes the stock is the same because the letters are the same type.

The Fix: Perform an Alphabetical Stock-Take. Sort the letters of both words into alphabetical order. Word A: [D-E-E-R-S-S-T]. Word B: [D-E-E-R-S-T]. The mismatch becomes instantly visible. If the counts don't match, the pair fails.
