A4 · Numerical Word Problems

Hidden Comparison

For parents

This type is deceptively simple in appearance but catches many children out. The question asks how many more one person or group has than another — but the phrasing makes children want to add rather than subtract. "How many more" often triggers addition automatically, even though the answer requires finding the difference between two numbers. The signal your child needs to catch is the word "more" appearing in the question alongside two quantities that need to be compared, not combined. When they see "how many more," the reflex should be: find the gap, not the total.

Signal card (read this to your child)

When a question asks "how many more" or "how many fewer" and gives you two amounts to compare — this is a Hidden Comparison question. Don't add. Find the gap between the two numbers. Subtract the smaller from the larger.

Opening sentences (child classifies — does not solve)

  1. Ava has 12 stickers. Ben has 7 stickers.
  2. There are 15 apples in a red bowl and 9 in a green bowl.
  3. Sam scored 14 points. Tom scored 8 points.
  4. A red car has 11 passengers. A blue car has 6.
  5. Lily read 16 pages. Her brother read 9 pages.
  6. There are 18 children in Year 3 and 11 in Year 2.
  7. A cat weighs 8 kg. A dog weighs 13 kg.
  8. Mum has 14 eggs. Gran has 6 eggs.
  9. A red tin has 20 buttons. A blue tin has 12.
  10. Jake collected 17 conkers. His friend collected 9.
  11. One shelf has 15 books. Another shelf has 7.
  12. A tall plant is 18 cm high. A short plant is 11 cm high.
  13. Class A has 25 pupils. Class B has 19 pupils.
  14. Ava saved £14. Ben saved £9.
  15. A yellow bag holds 13 marbles. A green bag holds 5.
  16. In the morning, 16 birds sat on a wire. In the afternoon, only 7 did.
  17. A school football team scored 11 goals this term. Last term they scored 6.
  18. A red box has 18 counters. A blue box has 12.
  19. Lily is 132 cm tall. Her sister is 118 cm tall.
  20. Tom's sunflower grew to 24 cm. Ava's grew to 17 cm.

Near-miss pairs (discrimination training)

AAva has 12 stickers. Ben has 7. How many more does Ava have?
BAva has 12 stickers. Ben gives her 7 more.
The difference: A compares two separate amounts — find the gap. B combines them — find the total.
ASam scored 14 points. Tom scored 8. How many more did Sam score?
BSam scored 14 points. Tom scored 8 more than Sam.
The difference: A finds the gap between two known values. B requires adding the excess to Sam's score to find Tom's total.
AOne shelf has 15 books. Another has 7. How many more books are on the first shelf?
BA shelf has 15 books. A pupil adds 7 more.
The difference: A compares two shelves — subtract. B adds to one shelf — add.
ALily read 16 pages. Her brother read 9. How many more did Lily read?
BLily had read some pages. Her brother read 9 fewer. Lily had read 16.
The difference: A is a straightforward comparison. B is still a comparison but framed from the other direction — same operation, different wording.
AClass A has 25 pupils. Class B has 19. How many more are in Class A?
BClass A has 25 pupils. Class B has 19. How many pupils are there altogether?
The difference: Same two numbers, opposite operations. "More" → subtract. "Altogether" → add.

Mastery check (parent reads aloud — child classifies only)

A4 Ava has 14 stickers. Ben has 9. How many more does Ava have?
A2 Ava has 14 stickers. Ben gives her 9 more. How many does she have now?
Discuss Ava has 14 stickers. Ben has 9. How many do they have altogether? — Same two numbers, but "altogether" means add, not subtract. What word tells you which operation to use?

For parents

The Hidden Comparison question asks for the difference between two quantities, but the language disguises this. "£8 less than he started with," "12 more than the other class," "how many fewer" — all of these signal a comparison, but the surface vocabulary often activates the wrong operation. The trap is particularly sharp when the comparison is expressed as a relationship to an unstated value: "now he has £8 less than he started with" requires identifying the starting value, computing the target, and subtracting. The classification signal: two quantities being related, not combined.

Signal card (read this to your child)

When a question compares two amounts using words like "more than," "fewer than," "less than," or "how many more/fewer" — this is a Hidden Comparison. The answer is the gap between the two amounts, not their total. Subtract the smaller from the larger.

Opening sentences (child classifies — does not solve)

  1. Class A has 34 pupils. Class B has 27 pupils.
  2. A red team scored 87 points. A blue team scored 64.
  3. A school library has 840 books. A nearby school's library has 612.
  4. A farmer grew 348 apples. His neighbour grew 279.
  5. Callum saved £48. His sister saved £31.
  6. A train carries 214 passengers. The next train carries 178.
  7. A shop sold 384 items on Saturday. On Sunday it sold 247.
  8. The school record for the long jump is 4.8 m. Maya jumped 3.9 m.
  9. Year 5 raised £324 for charity. Year 6 raised £287.
  10. A red car has travelled 1,240 km. A blue car has travelled 987 km.
  11. A builder laid 84 bricks before lunch. He laid 67 bricks after lunch.
  12. The morning session had 156 audience members. The afternoon session had 213.
  13. A factory produces 640 units on Monday. On Tuesday it produces 583.
  14. Tom's book has 312 pages. Maya's book has 248 pages.
  15. A swimming pool holds 480,000 litres. The school pool holds 124,000 litres.
  16. One side of a playground is 48 m long. The other side is 37 m long.
  17. A school collects 284 items for the food bank. The target was 350.
  18. A cyclist rides 67 km on Saturday. On Sunday she rides 84 km.
  19. The winning team scored 43 points. The runners-up scored 38.
  20. A school fete raises £1,240. Last year's fete raised £980.

Near-miss pairs (discrimination training)

AClass A has 34 pupils. Class B has 27. How many more are in Class A?
BClass A has 34 pupils. Class B joins them for assembly. There are now 61.
The difference: A compares — find the gap. B combines — find the total.
AYear 5 raised £324. Year 6 raised £287. How much more did Year 5 raise?
BYear 5 raised £324. Year 6 raised £287. How much did they raise altogether?
The difference: Same two numbers. "How much more" → subtract. "Altogether" → add.
AA shop sold 384 items on Saturday. On Sunday it sold 247. How many more did it sell on Saturday?
BA shop had some items. It sold 247 on Sunday. It had sold 384 altogether by end of weekend.
The difference: A is a direct comparison of two days' sales. B is start-unknown across two days — different structure entirely.
AA farmer grew 348 apples. His neighbour grew 279. How many more did the farmer grow?
BA farmer grew 348 apples. He gives his neighbour 279.
The difference: A compares two independent quantities. B subtracts from one quantity — direct operation.
AThe school record for the long jump is 4.8 m. Maya jumped 3.9 m. How much further is the record?
BMaya jumped 3.9 m. To beat the record of 4.8 m she needs to jump how much further?
The difference: Both compare the same two values — same calculation, different framing. The operation is identical despite the wording change.

Mastery check (parent reads aloud — child classifies only)

A4 Year 5 raised £324. Year 6 raised £287. How much more did Year 5 raise?
A2 Year 5 raised £324. Year 6 raised £287. How much did they raise altogether?
Discuss Year 5 raised some money. Year 6 raised £287, which was £37 less than Year 5. How much did Year 5 raise? — Comparison expressed as a relationship to an unknown. Must compute Year 5's amount from Year 6's amount plus the difference.

For parents

At this level, the Hidden Comparison is often expressed as a relational statement rather than a direct question. "The new model is 340 units more efficient than the old one," "output fell by 12% relative to last quarter," "the gap between the two schools narrowed to 43 points" — all describe a comparison without explicitly saying "subtract." The child who hasn't classified this type will often either add the comparison value, or treat it as the answer directly. The classification signal: a relationship between two quantities that defines a gap, not a total.

Signal card (read this to your child)

When a story describes a relationship between two quantities using "more than," "less than," "greater than," "the gap," or "relative to" — this is a Hidden Comparison. The answer is the difference between the two values. Identify both quantities and subtract.

Opening sentences (child classifies — does not solve)

  1. A company's Q1 revenue was £2.4 million. Q2 revenue was £1.87 million.
  2. The new factory produces 8,400 units per week. The old factory produced 6,750.
  3. School A's average test score is 74%. School B's average is 68%.
  4. A marathon runner's personal best is 3 hours 12 minutes. Her time today was 3 hours 27 minutes.
  5. The population of Town A is 84,200. The population of Town B is 67,800.
  6. A satellite's orbital period is 94.2 minutes. The ISS orbital period is 92.9 minutes.
  7. A cyclist's average speed on the outward journey was 24.6 km/h. On the return it was 21.3 km/h.
  8. The record rainfall for June is 84.3 mm. This June recorded 61.7 mm.
  9. A company's market share is 34.8%. Its closest competitor's is 28.4%.
  10. The winning candidate received 14,840 votes. The runner-up received 12,390.
  11. A school's budget increased from £1,240,000 to £1,380,000 over two years.
  12. A factory's energy consumption last year was 4,840 MWh. This year it is 4,120 MWh.
  13. The average house price in Area A is £384,000. In Area B it is £312,000.
  14. A swimmer's time in the heats was 58.4 seconds. In the final she swam 57.1 seconds.
  15. Exports this year totalled £84.3 billion. Last year they were £79.6 billion.
  16. The tallest building in the city is 312 m. The second tallest is 287 m.
  17. A school's carbon footprint fell from 840 tonnes to 612 tonnes over three years.
  18. The morning shift produced 1,240 components. The afternoon shift produced 987.
  19. A river's flow rate in summer is 84 cubic metres per second. In winter it is 312.
  20. The fastest lap was completed in 1 minute 24.3 seconds. The second fastest was 1 minute 26.8 seconds.

Near-miss pairs (discrimination training)

AA company's Q1 revenue was £2.4m. Q2 revenue was £1.87m. How much more was earned in Q1?
BA company's Q1 revenue was £2.4m. In Q2 it earned £1.87m more.
The difference: A compares two independent figures — subtract. B adds the Q2 figure to Q1 — add.
AThe winning candidate received 14,840 votes. The runner-up received 12,390. What was the margin of victory?
BThe winning candidate received 14,840 votes. The runner-up received 12,390 votes. How many votes were cast in total?
The difference: Same numbers. "Margin" → subtract. "Total" → add.
ASchool A's average score is 74%. School B's is 68%. What is the gap between them?
BSchool A's average score is 74%. After moderation, School B's score was revised upward by 6%.
The difference: A compares two independent scores. B applies a change to one score — direct addition.
AA factory's energy use last year was 4,840 MWh. This year it is 4,120 MWh. How much less is being used?
BA factory used 4,840 MWh last year. This year it reduced consumption by 720 MWh. What is this year's total?
The difference: A gives both values and asks for the difference. B gives the start and the reduction — subtract to find the end value. Different unknowns.
AA swimmer's heat time was 58.4 seconds. Her final time was 57.1 seconds. By how much did she improve?
BA swimmer's target time is 57.1 seconds. Her current personal best is 58.4 seconds. How much does she need to improve?
The difference: Both compare the same two values — same calculation, different framing. Make sure your child sees the operation is identical despite the different wording.

Mastery check (parent reads aloud — child classifies only)

A4 The winning candidate received 14,840 votes. The runner-up received 12,390. What was the margin of victory?
Discuss The winning candidate received 14,840 votes. The runner-up received 12,390 more votes than the third-place candidate, who received 4,200. — Multi-step: find the runner-up's total first, then compare to the winner.
A1 Together, the top two candidates received 27,230 votes. The winner received 14,840. How many did the runner-up receive? — Looks like a comparison but is actually a reversal. Discuss why.
