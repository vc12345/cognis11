Module 14: Interval Logic

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Interval Logic identifies the "Off-by-One" error that is a staple of 11+ competitive exams. When objects are placed in a row, the distance is not held by the objects themselves, but by the gaps between them. To solve these, you must always determine the number of intervals ($N - 1$) before performing any division or multiplication.

Advanced Year 2 "A clock strikes 4 times with 3 seconds between each strike. How long from start to finish?"
Average Year 4 "A 30m fence has posts every 6m. How many posts are used to build the straight fence?"
Advanced Year 5 "A doctor prescribes 6 pills to be taken at exactly 45-minute intervals. How much time passes between the first and last pill?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

The Boundary Map. Observe how the number of "Gaps" is always exactly one less than the number of "Objects." Calculations for total time or distance must always use the Gap count.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Fence Post" Trap: Dividing total distance by the interval length and assuming that is the number of posts. (e.g., A 20m fence with posts every 5m equals 4 posts). You have forgotten the starting anchor.

The Fix: Use the Anchor Rule. Division ($20 \div 5$) always gives you the number of Gaps. To find the number of objects, you must add 1 for the "Zero Post" at the very beginning. Remember: $Gaps + 1 = Objects$.
