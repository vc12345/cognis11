Module 2: Shared Ratios

Foundation Intro

The Coin Squash

Sharing a ratio is just like dealing cards. You have a big pile of coins, and you want to give them to two people fairly. The secret is that every box must hold the same amount. Once you know what's in one box, you can count the total for everyone.

"We have a pile of £60. The ratio is 2 : 1. That means we need 3 boxes total. Let's squash the money into those boxes so they are all exactly equal!"

The "Box Rule"

Tell your child: "The ratio tells you how many boxes to draw." If the ratio is 2:1, you draw 3 boxes. The total money always gets 'squashed' into the total number of boxes!

Foundation Progressing

The Gravity of Parts

Ratios have Gravity. If one side of the ratio is a bigger number, it is "heavier." It wins the tug-of-war and pulls more of the total money toward its side of the scale.

"Look at the scale. Side A has 4 parts and Side B only has 1. Because Side A has more parts, it is much heavier. It 'pulls' more of our £100 total into its pile!"

The "Tie-Breaker" Secret

The only time the scale is balanced is if the ratio is **1:1**. Any other ratio means one side is pulling harder. Always find the value of "One Part" first to see how much weight each single part adds!

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a ratio as a Chocolate Bar. To share it fairly, you don't just look at two people; you look at the total number of "chunks." The secret weapon for every ratio problem is finding the Value of One Chunk. Once your child knows what one single piece is worth, they can hand out the right amount to anyone.

The Sweet Shop "Share 15 sweets between Ben and Sam in the ratio 2:1. How many does Ben get?"
The Cash Prize "A £60 prize is split in the ratio 5:7. What is the larger share?"
The Ruler Challenge "A 1.5m line is divided 3:2. Find the difference between the two lengths."

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "One-Sided" Mistake: Children often try to divide the total by just one side of the ratio. They forget that the money covers the whole bar, not just one person's share.

The Fix: The "Add First" Rule. Remind your child: "Before you divide, you must find the Whole Bar." Add the ratio numbers together (A + B) first to see the total number of parts. Only then can you find the value of one chunk.
