Module 55: Fact vs. Opinion

Phase 1: The X-Ray

A Fact is a statement that can be proven true or false with data, a ruler, or a clock. An Opinion is a value judgment—a feeling or belief that cannot be measured. The logic of 11+ exams often hides opinions inside sentences that look like facts.

Advanced Year 2The sun is a star. (Fact)
The sun is pretty. (Opinion)
Average Year 4Everest is 8,848m high. (Fact)
Everest is the best mountain. (Opinion)
Advanced Year 5The 40-minute speech was a boring waste of time.
Mix: [Fact] + [Opinion]

Phase 2: The Anatomy

Parent Instruction: Use the "Prove It" test. Ask your child: "Could I prove this with a camera or a calculator?" If the answer is "No," it's an opinion. Words like should, best, beautiful, and worst are always opinions.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The Trap

The Expert Fallacy. If the text says, "The world-famous scientist said the movie was terrible," students often label "terrible" as a fact because a famous person said it. It remains an opinion regardless of who said it.

The Fix

The Quotation Shield. Any word that describes a feeling or uses a value-adjective (good, bad, difficult) is an opinion, even if it is inside a quote. If you can't measure it with a machine, it's not a fact.
