Module 55: Fact vs. Opinion

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Success in Reading Comprehension requires Empirical Verification. You must distinguish between Facts (statements that can be proven with data, clocks, or rulers) and Opinions (subjective value judgments). While facts are absolute, opinions are often "dressed up" as facts using authoritative language. To find the truth, ignore the tone and look for the measurement.

Advanced Year 2 "The sun is a star." (Fact) vs. "The sun is beautiful." (Opinion). Identify the measurable statement.
Average Year 4 "The 42-page report was far too long." Contrast the numerical fact with the subjective judgment.
Advanced Year 5 "The world-renowned expert argued that the policy was a failure." Is 'failure' a fact because an expert said it?

Phase 2: The Anatomy

The Verification Filter. Before accepting a statement, pass it through the "Machine Test." If a computer or a camera cannot confirm the word, it is an Opinion.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Expert Fallacy" Trap: Believing a statement is a fact just because an expert or a famous person said it. (e.g., "The Professor said the movie was terrible.") Because the Professor is a fact, you assume his judgment is a fact too.

The Fix: Use the Measurement Audit. If a word describes a feeling or uses a value-adjective (good, bad, difficult, boring), it is an opinion—even if it's inside a quote. If you cannot prove it with a machine or a record, it remains a personal belief.
