List A

The Deep Understander

Thinks before acting. Wants to know why, not just what.

✓
Asks "why" more than "what" — wants to understand how something works, not just what to do.
After learning that 3 × 4 = 12, asks: "But why does multiplying make it bigger?"
✓
Reads or listens carefully before starting — pauses to understand before acting.
In a board game, waits for all the rules before touching any pieces, even if others have started.
✓
Performs noticeably well on unfamiliar or unusual problems — ones that can't be pattern-matched.
A tricky word problem they haven't seen before often gets a better response than a fast drill question.
✓
Explains their reasoning clearly — can walk you through how they got an answer step by step.
"I knew it was addition because the total was missing, not one of the parts."
✓
Becomes bored or disengaged quickly during repetitive drilling — seems to plateau early.
After 8 or 9 of the same question type, visibly loses focus even when getting them right.
✓
Wants to understand why they got something wrong — won't just accept a correction and move on.
"But I don't understand — why is the answer 5p and not 10p?"
✓
Under time pressure, performance drops noticeably — more than you'd expect given their ability in relaxed conditions.
Gets most things right with no clock; runs out of time or makes careless errors toward the end of timed papers.
✓
When stuck, pauses and thinks before trying something — doesn't just guess and see what happens.
Sits quietly looking at a puzzle for 30 seconds before moving a single piece.
✓
Gets noticeably bothered by not knowing something — persists until they understand.
A single unexplained word will hold them up even when it isn't needed to understand the rest.
✓
When a rule is disputed, argues their position with reasoning — "but that doesn't make sense because…"
Insists on finding the correct answer rather than accepting a majority view and moving on.
List B

The Pattern Recogniser

Acts quickly. Trusts instincts. Matches to what they've seen before.

✓
Jumps into problems quickly — starts before finishing reading the question.
Begins writing an answer while you're still reading the problem aloud.
✓
Spots patterns quickly and confidently — seems to "just know" the answer without being able to explain how.
Looks at a number sequence and says "32" immediately, without being able to name the rule.
✓
Performs well under time pressure — speed feels natural, not stressful.
Timed tests don't seem to affect accuracy much — they finish with time to spare.
✓
Benefits noticeably from repetitive practice — scores improve steadily as the same question types recur.
After a week on the same question type, answers become noticeably faster and more confident.
✓
Accepts a correction and moves on without needing to understand why they were wrong.
"Oh, it's 5p — ok." Then immediately moves to the next question.
✓
Struggles more with unusual or novel questions — performs better on familiar formats.
A question phrased in an unfamiliar way throws them off, even if the underlying maths is easy.
✓
Prefers action over analysis — gets restless when asked to plan or reflect before doing.
In a group project, wants to start building immediately rather than spend time planning.
✓
Finds it hard to explain their working — gets the right answer but struggles to say how.
"I just knew it was 8." Can't reconstruct the steps even when the answer is correct.
✓
Moves on easily when they don't know something — doesn't dwell on gaps.
Encounters an unfamiliar word, skips it, keeps going without it seeming to bother them.
✓
Happy to learn rules as they go — will start a new game before fully understanding it.
Wants to start a new board game immediately; picks up the rules from the first round of play.
